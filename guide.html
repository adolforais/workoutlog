Hypertrophy Training Program: Comprehensive Guide

Overview and Purpose

This training program is meticulously designed to optimize muscle growth (hypertrophy) through structured progression, adequate volume, proximity to muscular failure, and recovery management. The routine is divided into six sessions per week, with each muscle group being trained at least twice weekly to maximize muscle protein synthesis.

Weekly Training Schedule

  • Monday: Push A (Chest, Front Delts, Triceps)
  • Tuesday: Pull A (Lats, Upper Back, Biceps)
  • Wednesday: Lower A (Quad-Dominant)
  • Thursday: Push B (Chest, Side Delts, Triceps)
  • Friday: Pull B (Upper-Back Thickness, Arms)
  • Saturday: Lower B (Posterior-Chain Bias)
  • Sunday: Rest

Exercise Selection and Variation

Each session includes compound exercises as the core movements for maximizing mechanical tension and isolation exercises for targeting specific muscles, achieving comprehensive muscular development.

Progression Model

This progression model is structured into four weekly microcycles:

Set Progression:
  • Week 1: Baseline sets from the program.
  • Week 2: Add 1 set to each starred isolation movement.
  • Week 3: Add another set to the same exercises.
  • Week 4: Deload week - remove one set from each exercise.
RIR Progression (Effort):
  • Week 1: 2-3 RIR (Reps In Reserve)
  • Week 2: 2 RIR
  • Week 3: 1 RIR
  • Week 4: 3-4 RIR (Deload)
Load Progression:
  • Use double progression: Keep weight constant until all sets reach top of rep range, then increase load.
  • Add 2.5–5 kg to compound lifts, and 1–2 kg to isolation movements when top reps are reached.
Failure Work:
  • Optional in Week 3 only, on final isolation sets. Never on compounds.
Block-to-Block:
  • Restart each new block slightly heavier than Week 1.
  • Monitor recovery before increasing set volume further.

This model ensures progressive overload while managing fatigue and joint stress.

Fatigue Management and Recovery

How to monitor recovery:
  • Track performance: you should be adding reps or load most weeks.
  • Energy: your motivation to train should remain high.
  • Soreness: muscle soreness should be tolerable and localized.
  • Sleep: consistent quality sleep is essential for repair.
Signs of overreaching:
  • Persistent fatigue or soreness
  • Trouble sleeping or irritability
  • No progress or loss of strength
Recovery safeguards:
  • Stick to the RIR targets - they are designed to balance stimulus and recovery.
  • Use Week 4 as a deload to restore performance.
  • Take extra rest days or reduce isolation work if needed.
Rest Between Sets:
  • Compounds: 90–120 sec
  • Isolation: 60–90 sec
Lifestyle Support:
  • Sleep: 7–9 hours per night
  • Protein: 1.6–2.2 g/kg body weight
  • Calories: mild surplus of 150–300 kcal over maintenance

Recovery is what converts training stress into muscle gains. Never compromise recovery for the sake of effort alone.

Volume vs. Intensity Trade-Off

Training Volume

  • Optimal range: 12–20 weekly hard sets per major muscle group
  • Larger muscles (quads, back, chest): aim for 16–20 sets
  • Smaller muscles (arms, delts, calves): 10–14 sets

Training Intensity

  • Work in the 6–15 rep range for hypertrophy
  • Most sets at 1–3 RIR
  • True failure only on isolation movements and only sparingly (Week 3)

Frequency

  • Train each muscle group at least twice per week
  • If you exceed 10–12 sets per muscle in one session, split the volume into two days

Practical Advice

  • Progress happens from cumulative weekly volume, not from one brutal workout
  • Don’t trade volume sustainability for frequent failure - fatigue will limit progress

The best results come from high-quality work, done consistently, with adequate recovery between sessions.

Practical Recommendations

Tracking and Logging:
  • Keep a workout log with exercises, sets, reps, load, and RIR
  • Review logs weekly to plan progression and catch plateaus early
Progressive Overload Strategy:
  • Add reps or load when top of rep range is reached with good form
  • Only increase difficulty when recovery markers are stable
Exercise Substitution:
  • If joint discomfort arises, replace a movement with a biomechanically similar one
  • Stick to the same substitutions for the rest of the block for consistency
Dealing with Plateaus:
  • Drop intensity slightly and build reps again
  • Insert a deload or reduce isolation work
  • Reassess sleep, calories, and total volume
When to Adjust the Program:
  • Persistently poor performance: take a full deload week
  • Lagging body part: add 1–2 sets per week for that muscle only
  • Excellent recovery and energy: you may extend a block to 6 weeks

A consistent, data-informed approach ensures your results don’t stall. Train smart, recover fully, and commit to every set with intent.

By strictly following this structured and evidence-based approach, you are set up for substantial and consistent muscular gains while mitigating risks associated with overtraining or injury.
