Below is a fresh hypertrophy‑only program that keeps the frequency you are used to (six training days, one rest day) while shifting every variable toward maximising muscle growth: higher weekly set volume, moderate‑to‑high rep ranges, short‑to‑moderate rest, and very small proximity to failure (1‑2 reps in reserve on most sets).

Why change?

  • Your last three months: A daily undulating P/P/L that mixed strength and size work. Heavy days (5‑6 reps) built strength, but total weekly sets for many muscles stayed near the lower end of the hypertrophy sweet spot.
  • Evidence update: Recent reviews still converge on roughly 12‑20 hard sets per muscle group per week as the most time‑efficient window for size gains in trained lifters. (PMC, BarBend)
  • Goal shift: To bias every stimulus toward size, we will 1) centre almost all work in the 6‑15 rep zone, 2) add specialised volume for lagging areas, and 3) use intensity techniques sparingly at the end of a session rather than loading very heavy early.

Progression model

  • Double progression: Pick a weight that lands mid‑rep range in week 1. Keep adding reps until you reach the top of the range for all sets, then add the smallest possible load and restart at the bottom.
  • Volume ramp: Weeks 1‑2 stay 2 RIR. Week 3 push most sets to 1 RIR. Week 4, maintain load but drop each exercise by one set (deload) before restarting at slightly heavier loads.
  • Intensity techniques: Limit drop sets and partials to the final isolation movement of the session to avoid accumulating too much fatigue.

Nutrition and recovery checklist

  1. Protein: 1.6‑2.2 g/kg body weight daily. (Science for Sport)
  2. Surplus: 150‑300 kcal above maintenance is usually enough for lean gains.
  3. Sleep: Aim for 7‑9 hours nightly.
  4. Deload: If joints or motivation sink, add an extra rest day or insert a full deload week earlier.

Key takeaways

  • All major muscle groups receive 16‑20 quality hypertrophy sets weekly, executed 1‑2 reps shy of failure, the zone most associated with maximal growth in recent meta‑analyses. (sportrxiv.org)
  • Nearly every main lift uses the 6‑15 rep range. This balances mechanical tension and metabolic stress while sparing joints from very heavy low‑rep work.
  • The push‑pull‑legs structure you enjoy remains, but exercise order, set count, and rep schemes are tuned for pure size.

Follow this for 8‑12 weeks, adjusting load or extra isolation volume if a body part still lags, then reassess before adding novel stimuli, like new exercises or different rep tempos. Consistency in training, eating, and sleeping will move the hypertrophy dial more than any single programming tweak.

4. Putting it all together

  1. Session level checklist
    • Choose the same load as last time.
    • Aim to add 1 rep to as many sets as possible until the top of the range is reached.
    • Keep rest periods consistent  2 min for compounds, 60‑90 s for isolation.
  2. Weekly review
    • If an exercise never climbs in reps for two weeks, drop the load by 5 percent and restart.
    • If you overshoot RIR targets (accidentally hit failure), reduce the load 2.5 kg next set.
  3. Block review every 4 weeks
    • After deload, reassess: most exercises will restart with 2.5‑5 kg more than the weight used in Week 1, giving measurable progress.
    • If a muscle is still lagging, add one extra isolation set in the next block for that muscle only.
  4. Intensity techniques
    • Limit drop sets, rest‑pause, or partials to one isolation exercise per session and only in Week 3.
    • Never use them on big compound lifts; the recovery cost outweighs the benefit.

Frequently asked questions

  • What if progress stalls mid‑block?
    Add a rest day, tighten sleep and calories, or trim one set from other body parts before adding more work.
  • Do I ever go to zero RIR?
    Only on small movements like curls, and only in Week 3, one to two sets max. The systemic fatigue is minimal, but the local stimulus is high.
  • Can I extend the block to six to eight weeks?
    Yes, if joints feel good and you keep gaining reps, but schedule a deload whenever bar speeds slow or motivation drops.

Key takeaway

By managing load, effort, and volume in a clear four‑week rhythm, you create a predictable overload without running into recovery debt. Your logbook will always tell you the next step: add a rep, add load, or back off. Follow the tables above for each lift, and you will keep driving hypertrophy for many months.
